# Chapter 10
## Second Companion

The corridor beyond the sanctum felt rougher.

Less curated. More like the dungeon had stopped trying to charm them and settled back into its default state: indifferent stone with teeth.

Rowan limped, jaw clenched, forcing a steady pace. The refusal had cost her. She could feel it—not in text, but in exhaustion. A heaviness in her limbs like she’d been carrying a backpack full of wet towels.

Kellan walked slightly ahead now, scanning corners. He had that post-correction edge to him, the kind of vigilance that came from knowing the world could change under your feet.

Lio kept close to Rowan, clutching the scrap of membrane like a talisman.

A sound drifted from ahead: a rhythmic clink. Metal against stone.

Rowan held up a hand. They stopped.

The sound repeated—steady, controlled. Not a creature. Not random.

Someone working.

They rounded the next curve and saw a narrow chamber carved into the corridor wall. Inside, hunched over something that glowed faintly, was a person with a hammer.

Not a blacksmith’s hammer. A small one, like a tool from a hardware store.

The person didn’t look up immediately. They kept tapping—careful, precise strikes—on a strip of metal laid across a stone block.

Rowan’s mouth went dry.

A memory: her dad’s garage when she was fourteen, the smell of oil and old wood, her dad showing her how to hold a wrench properly. “Don’t muscle it,” he’d said. “Line it up. Let the tool do the work.”

Rowan hadn’t thought about that in years.

The person finally looked up.

They were younger than Rowan—mid-twenties maybe—with dark hair pulled back messily and eyes that assessed like a scanner. Their clothes were patched and stained, but their hands were steady.

They saw Rowan’s limp. Lio’s bruises. Kellan’s tense posture.

Then their gaze flicked to empty air near Rowan’s shoulder, and their eyes narrowed.

They could see the tell, if not the text.

“Don’t move,” the person said, voice low.

Kellan’s shoulders tightened. “We’re not here to—”

“I didn’t say you were,” the person snapped. “I said don’t move.”

Rowan kept her hands visible. “Okay.”

The person straightened slowly and reached behind the stone block. They pulled out a crude spear—wood shaft, sharpened metal tip—and held it level.

Rowan swallowed. “We’re not looking for a fight.”

“Everyone says that,” the person said, flat.

Rowan nodded. “Fair.”

The person’s eyes stayed on Rowan. “You’re visible.”

Rowan felt a chill. “How do you know?”

The person’s mouth twitched. “Because you’re looking at something that isn’t there, and you’re not acting surprised about it.”

Rowan’s pulse thudded.

Kellan murmured, “Rowan—”

Rowan lifted a hand slightly, calming.

Rowan took a slow breath. “My name is Rowan Mercer.”

The person hesitated a fraction. “That’s a real name.”

Rowan almost smiled. “Yes.”

The person lowered the spear a millimeter. “Mine is Aster.”

Aster. Probably chosen. Probably armor. Still.

Rowan nodded. “Okay, Aster. We’re moving through. We got corrected back there.” She nodded vaguely toward the sanctum direction. “We’re trying not to get corrected again.”

Aster’s eyes flashed. “Then you’re failing.”

Rowan’s eyebrows lifted. “We’re alive.”

Aster snorted. “Alive is not the same thing as untracked.”

Rowan’s overlay flickered.

NEW ENTITY: ASTER
CLASSIFICATION: UNKNOWN
VISIBILITY: PARTIAL
NOTE: SYSTEM SIGNAL INTERFERENCE DETECTED

Rowan’s stomach tightened.

Interference.

Aster noticed Rowan’s micro-flinch and smiled—sharp, not kind, but not cruel either. A smile that said: I know what you see.

“You get text,” Aster said. “Right?”

Rowan didn’t deny it. “Yes.”

Aster’s gaze slid to Lio. “And they get… feelings.”

Lio flinched.

Rowan’s voice stayed calm. “Yes.”

Aster’s smile faded. “I hate that.”

Rowan blinked, surprised.

Aster's grip tightened on the spear. "I hate it because it's lazy. It's like—" They searched for words, then huffed. "It's like a bad manager who doesn't want documentation, so they just punish you until you 'get it.'"

Rowan's mouth opened slightly. Been there, done that. Got the t-shirt and the therapy bill.

Kellan muttered, almost to himself, “Oh. You’re one of us.”

Aster’s eyes narrowed. “Don’t.”

Rowan stepped forward a fraction. “What are you doing here?”

Aster glanced down at the metal strip on the stone block. “Making.”

“Making what?”

Aster lifted the strip. It had notches along one side—like a key, or a tool designed to fit into something specific. “A door tool.”

Rowan’s heart thudded. “For doors like the first one?”

Aster’s eyes flicked to Rowan’s ankle brace. “You’re past that.”

Rowan nodded. “Yes.”

Aster’s voice went quiet. “Then you know doors aren’t doors. They’re permissions.”

Rowan felt a grim smile tug at her mouth. “Yes.”

Aster studied her for a moment, then nodded once, as if making a decision.

“I can help you,” Aster said. “But not for free.”

Rowan’s shoulders tightened. “Name your price.”

Aster’s gaze flicked to Kellan. “You’re heavy.”

Kellan’s jaw clenched. “Excuse me?”

Aster ignored him and looked at Rowan. “Not in weight. In… debt. You feel like you’ve got a storm behind you.”

Rowan swallowed. “We do.”

Aster nodded. “Then my price is simple. You don’t bring that storm into my space.”

Rowan almost laughed. “That’s… not something I can promise.”

Aster’s eyes sharpened. “Then I’m not coming.”

Rowan considered. The system ledger. Interest pending. Correction likely. They needed someone who understood doors and interference.

They also needed someone who could say no.

Rowan nodded slowly. “Okay.”

Aster’s brows lifted. “Okay what?”

Rowan met their eyes. “Okay. You’re not joining us yet. But you can give us one thing—information.”

Aster stared, then let out a short laugh. “You’re negotiating while bleeding.”

Rowan shrugged. “It’s my hobby.”

Aster’s laughter died quickly, replaced by something like respect.

“What do you want to know?” Aster asked.

Rowan pointed with her chin at the metal strip. “How do you make a door tool?”

Aster hesitated. “It’s not just metal. It’s… alignment.”

Rowan waited.

Aster sighed. “The system expects compliance. Doors respond to patterns. Not just touch. Not just intention. Pattern.”

Rowan felt the idea click into place like a latch.

“Like an authentication token,” Rowan murmured.

Aster’s eyes widened slightly. “Yes.”

Rowan nodded. “And you’re forging a token.”

Aster’s mouth twitched. “When you put it that way, it sounds less mystical and more annoying.”

Rowan’s smile was real this time, brief. “Welcome to my entire life.”

Aster lowered the spear fully and reached behind the stone block again. They pulled out a small bundle and tossed it to Rowan.

Rowan caught it, startled.

Bandages. Better than what they had. A small vial with clear liquid.

Aster’s voice went flat. “Don’t make me regret that.”

Rowan blinked. “Why are you helping?”

Aster’s gaze shifted away. “Because you didn’t take the sanctum deal.”

Rowan’s stomach tightened. “How do you know?”

Aster shrugged. “People who take it come out… smooth. You’re still jagged.”

Rowan looked down at the bundle in her hands, then back at Aster.

Rowan nodded once. “Thank you.”

Aster grunted. “Don’t thank me. Just… don’t optimize.”

Kellan let out a breath that might have been a laugh.

Lio whispered, “Are you coming with us?”

Aster’s eyes flicked to Lio. For a moment, their expression softened.

Then the hardness returned. “Not yet.”

Rowan tucked the bundle under her arm. “If we survive long enough, we’ll come back.”

Aster’s gaze sharpened. “Don’t.”

Rowan paused. “Don’t what?”

Aster’s voice was low. “Don’t make promises you can’t keep. It’s how the system gets you.”

Rowan held their gaze.

Then she nodded slowly. “Fair.”

They backed out of the chamber and returned to the corridor.

As they walked away, Rowan glanced at her overlay.

DEBT: CURRENT LOAD 8
NOTE: SYSTEM SIGNAL INTERFERENCE (ASTER) MAY REDUCE COLLECTION EFFICIENCY

Rowan’s pulse thudded.

Aster wasn’t just a companion.

Aster was a bug in the system.

And Rowan had just made contact.
