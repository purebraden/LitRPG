## The City

The city is organized for efficiency. The System prefers clean geometries, predictable routes, and clear boundaries. Every district serves a purpose. Every wall contains. Every watchtower observes.

Rowan's camp lies one mile outside the containment wall—close enough to observe, far enough to remain illegible. For now.

---

## Key Locations

**Central Plaza** — The heart of the city. Where corrections are performed publicly. Where the System makes examples.

**Market District** — Where probability bends. Where small failures accumulate. Where residual permissions stack.

**Correction Facility** — Where the System processes deviations. Where cases are built. Where people are remade.

**Admin Complex** — Where the System speaks through proxies. Where containment logic is calculated. Where decisions are deferred.

**Factory Sector** — Where efficiency is measured. Where optimization is rewarded. Where people become functional.

**Underground Access Tunnels** — Where illegibility hides. Where the System's vision fails. Where escape becomes possible.

**Containment Wall** — Where the city ends. Where surveillance begins. Where the System draws the line.

**System Watchtowers** — Where observation never stops. Where patterns are recognized. Where deviations are flagged.

**Rowan's Camp** — Where resistance begins. Where the System's reach thins. Where humanity persists.

**River** — Where the city's influence wanes. Where the System's control weakens. Where healing becomes possible.

**Ruins & Wilderness** — Where the System never fully claimed. Where variance survives. Where memory remains.

# Prologue
## Audit Window Opened

The first thing Rowan noticed was the silence.

Not the kind you earned after a long day—the soft hush where the world exhales and you can pretend you're safe. This silence sat wrong. It made her check her ears, then her pulse, then the angle of her head like she could tilt herself back into normal sound.

The camp had gone quiet in a way that felt deliberate. Her fire still burned, a ragged orange tongue licking at damp wood, but it wasn't crackling the way it should. Even the sparks seemed reluctant to leave, as if they'd been told to stay put.

Rowan held her hands over the flame anyway. Heat seeped into her knuckles slow and grudging, not the bright, eager warmth she'd come to rely on. Across the fire, a small kettle perched on three stones. The water inside barely trembled. She'd set it there before dark because having a plan—boil, pour, sip—gave her something to do with her hands while her brain ran laps.

It had worked then. It wasn't working now.

She reached for the tin cup and stopped halfway.

A thin film of frost had crawled up the cup's side. It shouldn't have. The night wasn't that cold. The air had been damp and mild enough earlier that her hair stuck to the nape of her neck.

Frost meant the System was leaning on the world.

Rowan pulled her cloak tighter and forced herself to breathe through her nose. The air smelled like wet soil and smoke and something faintly metallic, like blood on old coins.

On her left, Lio shifted near the fire. He sat close enough to the heat that the edges of his sleeves steamed faintly, drying where they'd gotten damp from fog. He didn't look at her right away. He was busy with a familiar ritual: feeding the fire in small, careful pieces like he could talk the night into behaving.

He slid a twig in. Waited. Listened.

Then, quietly, he said, "You're doing the thing where you stop blinking."

Rowan blinked. Once. Twice. Her eyes stung from staring too long. "I'm fine."

Lio made a soft sound—half skepticism, half acceptance. He didn't argue. That was his gift and his curse. "Do you want the last of the dried fruit?"

Rowan looked down at the pouch by her knee. The leather was stiff with salt. She'd been saving it, because saving things made her feel she had a future.

"No," she said. "You can have it."

"Mm," Lio replied, not agreeing, not refusing. He tossed another stick into the fire and watched it catch reluctantly.

On the other side of the camp, Kellan paced the perimeter in a slow, irritated loop. He walked like he was trying to keep his muscles from remembering fear. Every few steps, he paused, cocked his head, and stared into the trees.

Not because he heard something.

Because he didn't.

Kellan stopped behind Rowan, close enough that his shadow fell across her shoulder. "It's too quiet."

Rowan kept her eyes on the frost line creeping along the cup. "Yeah."

Kellan's boots crunched softly on dead leaves. He came around, crouched on the opposite side of the fire, and put his forearms on his knees. Firelight sharpened his cheekbones and made his eyes look darker than they were. "Don't 'yeah' me. Say what you're thinking."

Rowan's mouth went dry. "I'm thinking it's the System."

Kellan's jaw tightened like that was both obvious and unacceptable. "And?"

Rowan wanted to say it can't do anything new. She wanted to believe the System was a machine that only repeated what it already knew. But she'd watched it learn. She'd watched it adapt—slowly, clumsily, but undeniably.

She'd watched it react to Wildcard by refusing to admit Wildcard existed at all.

"It's leaning closer," Rowan said.

Lio's hands paused over the firewood. "How can you tell?"

Rowan nodded toward the cup without looking up. "That."

Lio leaned in, eyes narrowing. Frost. His mouth tightened. "That wasn't there before."

Kellan followed Rowan's gaze, then looked up at the empty air above the fire the way you look at a camera you can't see. "Is it going to do something?"

Rowan didn't answer fast enough.

A notification chimed.

Not sound—pressure. A tiny pulse behind her eyes, like someone had tapped the inside of her skull. Rowan's vision blurred for half a second, and then the UI slid into place with a smoothness that made her stomach drop.

She didn't look up right away.

She watched her hands—pale in the firelight, soot in the creases of her thumb—and waited for the familiar spike of nausea that came with the System's attention.

It didn't come.

That was worse.

The UI appeared without pain. Efficient. Polished. Like someone had patched the rough edges she'd gotten used to.

Rowan lifted her eyes.

A thin gray bar hung in the air, perfectly level, perfectly centered.

[SYSTEM]
NOTICE: COMPLIANCE AUDIT INITIATED
Subject: Rowan Mercer
Scope: Behavioral Consistency / Outcome Divergence
Window: OPEN
Observer: [REDACTED]
[/SYSTEM]

Kellan swore under his breath. Lio went very still, his fingers still holding a stick of wood as if it mattered.

Rowan's throat tightened. She forced her voice into something steady. "Let me read."

Kellan's laugh was sharp and humorless. "As if we have a choice."

A second pane slid in beneath the first, text dense enough to make her eyes want to skim and classify.

[SYSTEM]
Audit Trigger: Cumulative anomaly threshold exceeded.
Category: Choice Pattern Deviation
Flag(s):
• Non-Optimizing Action Chain
• Undocumented Mechanic Interaction
• Persistent Residual State
• Unresolved Observer Gap
[/SYSTEM]

Rowan's tongue stuck to the roof of her mouth.

Unresolved observer gap.

She'd seen that absence once—the moment the world had stuttered at the edge of her vision and the System insisted the space was empty even while a person moved through it like they belonged to a different set of rules.

Wildcard.

They'd all been there, clustered too close together in the wet dark, pretending they weren't desperate for an answer. Wildcard had looked at them like they were a problem that would either solve itself or kill them.

You're loud, Wildcard had said, eyes flicking to Rowan. Even when you think you're quiet.

Lio's voice cut in softly, almost reluctantly. "That's… about them."

Rowan nodded once, too small to feel like agreement.

Kellan leaned forward until the fire reflected in his eyes. "Tell me you didn't get tagged just for standing in the same clearing."

Rowan stared at the floating words. "It thinks I'm connected."

"Are you?" Kellan's question came out too fast. Too sharp. Like he could stab the answer into being safe.

Rowan opened her mouth and closed it again. Honesty mattered. So did survival.

"I talked," she said finally. "We all did. That's all."

Kellan exhaled hard through his nose. "That's never 'all' with this thing."

The UI flickered, as if irritated by their tone.

Another line appeared beneath the flags, clinical and uncaring.

[SYSTEM]
Audit Action: Retroactive evaluation of recent decisions.
Audit Consequences: Adjustment of rewards / encounter probability / social alignment metrics.
Note: Subject may experience perception latency, UI throttling, and environmental stabilization attempts.
[/SYSTEM]

Lio's eyes went to the trees. The fog between the trunks sat too flat, too unmoving, like a sheet of cloth hung in place. "Environmental stabilization," he murmured. "That's the frost."

Kellan's fists clenched. "So it can just punish you."

"It can adjust," Rowan said, and the word tasted like corporate euphemism. Adjustment. Like a performance review that decided your rent.

Kellan scoffed. "Same thing."

Rowan's mind tried to do what it always did when she was afraid: build a model. Monitoring. Alerting. Incident response. You didn't fix an outage by staring at the graphs until they behaved. You identified what changed. What was failing. How fast it was spreading.

Okay.

What changed?

Book one had been chaos—drop-in, scramble, survive. She'd refused the easy prompts. She'd pushed back in small ways that felt private. She'd made choices the System didn't reward because rewarding them would encourage others to try.

Then it had gone quiet.

Waiting was never good.

Kellan's voice pulled her back. "Rowan."

She realized she'd been staring at the same line of text for too long. "I'm thinking."

"Don't do that alone," Kellan snapped, then softened it with effort. "Don't go somewhere we can't reach you."

The words hit harder than he intended. Rowan turned her head. Kellan looked angry in the way people looked when fear had nowhere else to go.

Lio, still by the fire, didn't move closer, but his attention locked on her like a hand on the back of her neck. Present. Steady.

Rowan nodded once. "I'm here."

Lio asked, gentle but not indulgent, "What do you need?"

The offer was simple. It made something in Rowan's chest ache. She rubbed her thumb over the pad of her index finger—an old habit from long meetings and long nights and pretending she wasn't scared.

"I need you both to not do anything sudden," she said.

Kellan's eyebrows lifted. "Sudden?"

Rowan gestured vaguely at the floating panes. "It's watching for patterns. If we act like frightened prey, it files that. If we act like heroes, it files that. If we act as if we're gaming it—"

"It files that," Kellan finished, bitterness thick in his voice.

Rowan's mouth twisted. "And then it adapts."

Lio's gaze flicked to her face. "So we do nothing?"

"No." Rowan shook her head. "We do… boring."

Kellan gave a short, incredulous laugh. "Boring gets you killed out here."

"Boring gets you missed," Rowan said, and immediately regretted how close that sounded to Wildcard's world.

Kellan caught it anyway. His eyes narrowed. "That's what they do."

Rowan didn't answer. She didn't trust her voice.

The air behind her eyes pulsed again. A third pane unfolded, longer than the others, lined like a contract. Rowan felt her stomach dip as it scrolled.

[SYSTEM]
AUDIT CHECKLIST: RECENT ACTIONS
Item 01: Refusal of optimization prompt (x3) — Under Review
Item 02: Non-combat resolution of encounter (x2) — Under Review
Item 03: Delay in quest acceptance — Under Review
Item 04: Unauthorized interaction with system-adjacent entity — Under Review
Item 05: [REDACTED] — Unresolved
Item 06: [REDACTED] — Unresolved
Item 07: Observer gap proximity event — Under Review
[/SYSTEM]

Lio sucked in a slow breath. "It's going through you."

"Like a ledger," Kellan said, voice flat.

Rowan couldn't stop staring at Item 05 and 06. The redactions weren't black bars. They were blankness, as if the words had never existed. As if the System didn't even have placeholders for what it couldn't categorize.

Her scalp prickled. The fire popped softly—one tiny spark rose and died without sound.

Rowan whispered, "It's missing data."

Kellan's mouth twisted. "Good."

"No." The word came out sharper than she meant. Rowan swallowed and tried again. "Missing data makes it nervous."

Lio tilted his head. "Why?"

Rowan tried to explain without slipping into lecture. Without making herself a narrator. "Because… it can't predict what happens next."

Kellan's gaze flicked to the fog again, and this time he looked like he could see the pressure in the air. "So it tightens the world."

Environmental stabilization attempts.

The frost had spread beyond the cup now, creeping along the closest branches in thin white lace. The fog sat low and unmoving, a flat sheet between trunks. It didn't curl or drift. It just waited, like a held breath.

Rowan rubbed her palms on her pants. They were damp with sweat despite the chill.

Lio's voice went quiet. "Rowan. What does it want from you?"

Rowan's eyes snapped back to the UI.

A new line had appeared at the bottom of the audit pane, understated but unmistakable.

[SYSTEM]
Acknowledgment Required.
Proceed?
[YES] [LATER]
[/SYSTEM]

Rowan stared at the buttons like they were mines.

If she hit YES, she stepped into whatever the System had prepared. If she hit LATER, she admitted fear—or worse, she created a delay that would be recorded as noncompliance. She could almost feel it slotting her choice into a column.

Her heartbeat thudded in her ears, the only real sound in the too-quiet night.

Kellan's hand hovered near his belt knife. Not drawing it. Just remembering it existed.

Lio's hands were open on his knees, palms up, like he was telling his body to stay calm even if his mind didn't believe it.

Rowan's mouth tasted like copper. She looked down at her hands again just to anchor herself. Nails bitten too short. A smear of soot on her thumb. Real. Human.

In her old life, audits had been paperwork and polite lies. There'd been calendars and timeboxes and carefully phrased emails with "per our last conversation" tucked like knives inside courtesy.

This audit had teeth.

Rowan thought of Wildcard moving through impossible empty space. Thought of the System refusing to acknowledge them. Thought of the way the UI had stuttered like a program hitting an unhandled exception.

Loud even when you think you're quiet.

Maybe Wildcard had meant behavior. Maybe Wildcard had meant emotion. Maybe Wildcard had meant the way Rowan couldn't hide the fact that she cared, even when caring was a vulnerability.

A sharp, unwanted longing cut through her—clean and bright as glass. Her bed. The dull hum of a refrigerator. The weight of her daughter's sleepy body against her shoulder, warm and unthinking. The sound of a world that didn't grade every breath she took.

The longing spiked—

—and the UI trembled.

Not much. A tiny jitter at the edge of the pane, like it had to re-center its focus. Like it had flinched.

Rowan froze.

Lio noticed immediately. His gaze snapped from the fire to her face. "What?"

Rowan didn't answer. She watched the UI like it might betray itself again.

A thin line of text appeared, faint enough she almost missed it.

[SYSTEM]
Perception latency detected. Recalibrating.
[/SYSTEM]

Her pulse kicked. She forced her face to stay blank.

Perception latency.

The System was lagging.

Kellan leaned in, voice low. "It's… what? Glitching?"

Rowan swallowed. "Not exactly."

Lio's brows drew together. "Rowan."

Rowan looked at them both, and the fear in her chest shifted—not lessening, but sharpening into something usable. "When I said boring," she said, "I didn't mean empty."

Kellan frowned. "You're doing it again. Speaking in riddles."

"I'm noticing," Rowan insisted. "It can track what we do. It's good at that. But it might not be as good at tracking what we—" She searched for the words that didn't sound like a manual. "What we carry. What we feel."

Kellan's eyes narrowed. "That's your new theory?"

Rowan didn't like how small it sounded when spoken aloud. "It's not a theory. It's… a crack."

Lio's voice stayed gentle, but it didn't let her drift away. "Okay. A crack. What do we do with it?"

The buttons waited, patient as a trap.

Rowan's finger lifted, hovering in the air between YES and LATER. She didn't trust either option. She didn't like being forced into binary choices. The System loved binaries. The System ate them.

So Rowan did something small and stubborn and human.

She reached into her pocket and pulled out the strip of dried fruit she'd been saving. It was leathery and sour and too expensive to waste. She tore it into three pieces with her teeth because her hands were shaking just enough that she didn't trust them.

She held one out to Kellan. One to Lio. Took the smallest for herself.

Kellan stared at it like she'd offered him a spell. "Now?"

"Yes," Rowan said. Her voice came out rougher than she meant. "Eat."

Lio accepted his piece without comment, as if he understood the point before she did. He chewed slowly, eyes half-lidded, letting the taste pull him into the moment.

Kellan hesitated, then took it. His fingers brushed hers—cold, calloused. He popped it into his mouth and grimaced at the sourness.

"God," he muttered. "That's awful."

"It's real," Rowan said, and surprised herself with the way her throat tightened. "It tastes like something. I need us to do something that isn't for it."

For a heartbeat, they were just three people by a fire, sharing a small cruelty of flavor and laughing at it without sound.

The UI flickered again. A faint ripple ran through the audit pane like a screen refresh.

Rowan kept chewing. Kept breathing. Kept her face bland and uninteresting while her chest stayed full of that sharp, dangerous wanting.

Then she selected LATER.

The button dimmed as if pressed by an invisible finger. The UI didn't vanish. It didn't punish her immediately. It simply changed.

[SYSTEM]
Acknowledgment deferred.
Audit Window: ACTIVE (00:59:59)
Note: Continued anomaly accumulation may result in forced evaluation.
[/SYSTEM]

A countdown appeared beneath it, numbers too clean, too bright. The colon blinked like an eye.

Kellan exhaled. "So we have an hour."

Lio's gaze stayed on the fog. "Or it has an hour."

Rowan nodded once. Her tongue was still sour. Her hands were still warm from the fire, and she clung to that warmth like proof she wasn't entirely made of fear.

An hour was nothing. An hour was enough time for the System to rearrange an entire region if it decided the variable needed containment.

Rowan looked at the redacted lines again—Item 05, Item 06—and felt, suddenly, like she was standing on a floorboard that hadn't been nailed down properly. Like the world beneath her could tilt at any moment.

Kellan's voice went quieter. "If it forces evaluation… what happens?"

Rowan watched the frost creep farther along the branch tips. A small bird sat motionless on a low limb, feathers puffed, eyes closed. It looked asleep. Or frozen. The fog didn't move. The fire still didn't crackle.

Rowan answered honestly. "I don't know."

Kellan's jaw flexed. "That's not—"

"I don't know," Rowan repeated, and the fear finally showed in the tremor of her breath. She hated it. She let it happen anyway. "But I think it wants me to become predictable."

Lio's eyes flicked to her face, seeing too much. "And will you?" he asked.

Rowan looked at them. Really looked.

Kellan's anger, sharp-edged and protective. Lio's quiet steadiness, the way he'd taken the fruit without needing a speech. The fact that they'd all stood with Wildcard and left with no answers, only warnings.

A warmth stirred in Rowan's chest—small, fierce, almost painful. Caring made her loud. Caring made her visible.

The UI's edge shimmered again, a faint stutter like a skipped frame.

Rowan kept her face steady. "No," she said softly. "But I might have to learn how to be unpredictable without dragging you into the worst of it."

Kellan let out a short laugh that didn't have humor in it. "That's not reassuring."

Rowan's mouth twitched. "Yeah. I know."

Lio leaned forward and nudged the kettle a fraction closer to the heat. "Then we do it together," he said, like it was the simplest thing in the world. Like it was obvious. "No more disappearing into your head."

Rowan swallowed hard and nodded once, because if she tried to speak, her voice might break.

The countdown ticked on, bright and indifferent.

Rowan reached for another piece of wood and fed it into the flames.

It caught slowly, reluctant—like the world was waiting to see what she'd do next.

# Chapter 1: Residual State Detected

The city announced itself before it appeared.

Not with walls or gates or banners, but with a feeling Rowan couldn’t quite name—like stepping into a room where the air had already been measured and found acceptable. The road smoothed beneath their feet. The mud thinned, then vanished entirely, replaced by stone worn flat by traffic that moved the same way every day, at the same pace, with the same purpose.

Rowan noticed she was breathing shallower.

Ahead of them, buildings rose in neat, confident lines. Not tall enough to inspire awe, not cramped enough to feel desperate. Windows repeated at comfortable intervals. Streets curved gently instead of cutting sharp angles. Everything about it suggested compromise, moderation, a thousand small optimizations layered until nothing stood out.

“This place feels…” Lio trailed off, searching.

“Safe,” Kellan supplied. He sounded relieved. “Too safe, maybe, but I’ll take it.”

Rowan didn’t answer. The word sat wrong in her chest.

Safe meant predictable. Predictable meant measurable.

They crossed the threshold without ceremony. No guards stopped them. No banners declared jurisdiction. The road simply became part of the city, and the city accepted them the way a form accepts new data—quietly, without interest.

The soundscape changed first. Footsteps echoed in controlled rhythms. Voices carried just far enough to be understood, then faded. Somewhere nearby, metal rang—too regular to be accidental. Rowan tried to pick out something irregular, something out of place.

She couldn’t.

People moved around them in tidy streams. No jostling. No raised voices. A woman carrying a basket adjusted her grip at the exact moment a child darted past, never breaking stride. Two men argued softly near a fountain, their disagreement resolving itself before Rowan caught the substance of it.

Resolution without friction.

Rowan rubbed her thumb against her forefinger, a habit she hadn’t shaken from her old life. Something here reminded her of dashboards and green checkmarks and systems that only screamed after it was already too late.

Lio walked a little closer to her without comment. Kellan drifted a step ahead, scanning the street like he expected danger to jump out and announce itself.

Instead, a voice did.

“You’re new.”

The woman who stepped into their path smiled easily, like she’d practiced it in a mirror and decided it worked. She wasn’t blocking the road so much as redefining it, angling herself so stopping felt natural.

“I’m Tara,” she said. “If you’re looking for the intake hall, you overshot it.”

Kellan frowned. “We weren’t looking for anything.”

Tara laughed—not sharply, not defensively. Just a warm, imperfect sound. “That’s what everyone says.”

Rowan watched her closely. Tara’s clothes were practical but mismatched. Her hair refused to stay tied back. One sleeve had been repaired twice, badly. She shifted her weight when she stood, never quite settling into stillness.

Unoptimized.

“I can show you around,” Tara continued. “Or not. But if you’re planning to stay longer than a night, you’ll want to know which districts pretend to be friendly and which ones actually are.”

Lio smiled before Rowan realized he would. “That would help.”

Kellan shot him a look. “We don’t know her.”

Tara raised an eyebrow. “That’s true. But I know you’re carrying gear you don’t want confiscated, and I know the east ward asks fewer questions if you don’t make eye contact.”

Kellan’s jaw tightened. Rowan felt the familiar urge to intervene, to smooth the moment before it escalated. Instead, she watched Tara.

Tara didn’t press. She just waited.

The pause stretched. It should have been awkward. It wasn’t.

“Fine,” Kellan said finally. “We walk. You talk. No detours.”

Tara grinned. “Deal.”

As they moved deeper into the city, Rowan’s unease sharpened. The streets were clean without being sterile. Stalls offered food that smelled rich and filling. People smiled when spoken to.

But the smiles arrived a fraction too late.

A merchant greeted Tara by name. His voice was friendly, his words welcoming—but Rowan saw the delay. A half-beat between Tara’s wave and his response, like he’d waited for permission to react.

She felt it then. A tug behind her eyes. Not pain—pressure.

Rowan stumbled.

Lio caught her elbow instantly. “Hey.”

“I’m fine,” Rowan said automatically, then stopped. The lie tasted wrong. “I just… give me a second.”

Tara turned, concern creasing her brow. “You sick?”

Rowan shook her head. The pressure intensified, resolving into something familiar and dangerous.

Empathy.

She hadn’t reached for it. She hadn’t decided. It bloomed anyway, a residual echo stirred by proximity.

Rowan’s vision blurred—not fading, but doubling. The merchant’s face stayed where it was, smiling patiently, while something else slid underneath.

Waiting.

That was the first thing she felt. Endless, procedural waiting. Not boredom. Not calm. Waiting as a state of being, stretched thin and polished smooth.

She gasped.

Lio’s voice came from far away. “Rowan?”

The merchant spoke again, repeating his greeting word for word. His mouth moved a fraction before the sound emerged.

Rowan tore herself free.

The pressure snapped, leaving her breathless and shaking. The world rushed back in too fast—noise, color, heat. Her knees buckled.

Lio lowered her carefully to the edge of the fountain. His hands were steady. “You’re not fine.”

“I know,” Rowan whispered. Her head throbbed. It felt like she’d sprinted for miles and then been asked to solve a puzzle at gunpoint.

Tara crouched in front of her. “What happened?”

Rowan forced herself to meet Tara’s eyes. “How long has he been like that?”

Tara blinked. “Like what?”

The merchant smiled. Waited.

Rowan swallowed. “Never mind.”

The aftermath hit hard and fast. Her limbs felt heavy, her thoughts thick. Time slipped. Minutes bled into each other. The city moved on around them, unconcerned.

Kellan paced once, sharp and restless. “We should move.”

“Give her a moment,” Lio said quietly.

Rowan tried to stand. Failed. The cost pressed down on her, undeniable.

Aftermath time.

She leaned back against the stone and closed her eyes, just for a second. Fear that wasn't hers surfaced first—followed by relief at not having to choose, then the soft terror of being guided gently into stillness.

Her stomach twisted.

When she opened her eyes again, Tara was watching her differently. Not scared. Not suspicious. Just… attentive.

“You do that a lot?” Tara asked.

Rowan laughed weakly. “No.”

“Good,” Tara said. “Because this city doesn’t like things it can’t account for.”

Kellan stopped pacing. “What do you know about accounting?”

Tara shrugged. “Enough to keep my head down.”

Kellan turned to Rowan, his expression tight with calculation. "How does this place work?"
He hesitated, then added, "What does it reward?"

Rowan opened her mouth, then closed it. The question felt too close to asking the System to explain itself. "I don't know yet."

Kellan's jaw flexed. "We need to know."

As if summoned by the word, Rowan felt it—a subtle shift in the air. Attention. The city didn't change, not visibly. But something aligned.

Across the square, two figures in matching cloaks turned at the same time—not toward her, but toward the space she occupied.

System attention.

Rowan closed her eyes and breathed through the fog. One accidental use. Hours lost. The System leaning closer.

This was just the beginning.

When she finally stood, with Lio’s help, the city felt heavier. Less welcoming. Like a hand settling between her shoulder blades.

She looked at Tara. At Kellan, already calculating. At Lio, grounding and present.

The state didn't fade when the pressure did.

Rowan stepped forward anyway.

# Chapter 2: Companions Are Not Constants

Tara led them to a small room above a bakery that smelled like yeast and old wood. The space was cramped but clean, with three narrow beds pushed against the walls and a single window that overlooked a courtyard where people moved in predictable patterns.

Rowan sat on the edge of her bed, watching the courtyard below. A woman swept the same patch of stone three times, each pass identical. A man adjusted a sign, stepped back, adjusted it again, stepped back. No one seemed to notice the repetition. No one seemed to mind.

"Food's here," Lio said, setting a tray on the small table between the beds. Steam rose from bowls of thick soup and a loaf of bread that looked like it had been baked to specification—uniform, golden, perfect.

Kellan didn't move from his post by the door. "How do we know it's safe?"

Tara, who'd been leaning against the window frame, laughed softly. "You don't. But I've been eating here for months, and I'm still me."

"That's not proof," Kellan said.

"No," Tara agreed. "But it's data. You want proof, you'll be hungry a long time."

Lio picked up a bowl and handed it to Rowan. "Eat. You look like you haven't slept."

Rowan accepted it without looking away from the window. The soup was warm, rich with vegetables that tasted like they'd been grown in a lab—nutritious, balanced, slightly off. She forced herself to take another spoonful.

Tara moved closer, her shadow falling across Rowan's lap. "You're doing it again."

Rowan looked up. "Doing what?"

"Noticing." Tara's eyes were sharp, too perceptive. "You've been cataloging the city since we walked in. The way people move. The timing. The patterns."

Rowan's voice caught. "Is that a problem?"

"Depends," Tara said. "If you're just curious, fine. If you're planning something, that's different. This city doesn't like plans it didn't approve."

Kellan's voice cut in, hard. "What does that mean?"

Tara shrugged, but her shoulders were tense. "It means if you look too closely, the city looks back. And it doesn't like what it sees."

Rowan set her bowl down. The soup had gone cold faster than it should have. "What happens if it doesn't like what it sees?"

Tara's expression shifted—something between sympathy and warning. "It helps you see things differently."

Lio sat on the bed next to Rowan's, his own bowl balanced on his knees. "Tara's been here longer than us. If she says to be careful, we should listen."

Kellan's jaw flexed. "We don't know her."

"I trust her," Lio said simply.

Rowan watched the exchange, feeling the weight of it. Lio had always been quick to trust—too quick, Kellan would say. But Lio's instincts were usually right. He read people the way Rowan read systems: patterns, tells, the small signals that betrayed intent. The kind of thing you learned after enough on-call shifts, when false positives taught you to trust your gut even when the metrics looked clean.

Tara noticed Rowan watching. "You don't trust me."

It wasn't a question.

Rowan chose her words carefully. "I don't trust easily. That's not about you."

Tara's mouth twitched. "Fair. But you're going to have to decide. Because if you keep looking at this city like it's a problem to solve, it's going to solve you first."

The words hit like a physical blow. Rowan's hands curled into fists.

Kellan stepped away from the door. "What the hell does that mean?"

Tara didn't flinch. "It means the System here is subtle. It doesn't force. It suggests. It optimizes. And if you're not optimized, it helps you get there."

Rowan stood, her legs unsteady. "I need air."

"Rowan—" Lio started.

"I'm fine." She moved toward the door, but Tara blocked her path.

"Wait." Tara's voice softened. "I'm not trying to scare you. I'm trying to warn you. This place… it's not evil. It's just efficient. And efficiency doesn't leave room for questions."

Rowan's pulse thudded in her ears. "I have questions."

"I know," Tara said. "That's why I'm worried."

Rowan pushed past her and opened the door. The hallway was empty, quiet. She walked down the stairs, through the bakery's front room, and out into the street.

The city breathed around her. People moved in their patterns. The air smelled like bread and something else—something clean and chemical, like disinfectant that had been scented to seem natural.

Rowan walked without direction, letting her feet carry her. She needed to think. She needed to understand what Tara was really saying. Her mind fell into familiar patterns—assessing, cataloging, building a mental model. The same process she'd used during production incidents, when you had to understand the system before you could fix it.

Ahead, a small square opened up. A fountain sat in the center, water cascading in perfect arcs. Around it, people sat on benches, talking in low voices. Their conversations overlapped, but never interrupted. No one raised their voice. No one gestured too broadly.

Rowan found an empty bench and sat, watching.

A woman approached the fountain, a child's hand in hers. The child pointed at the water, asking something Rowan couldn't hear. The woman smiled—warm, patient, perfect.

Rowan felt it then. The pressure behind her eyes. The familiar tug of empathy wanting to reach out.

She'd felt it before, accidentally. This time, she made a choice.

Rowan let the empathy bloom.

The world doubled. The woman's face stayed where it was, smiling down at her child, while something else slid underneath.

Rowan expected emotion. Fear, joy, frustration, love—something human.

She found nothing.

Not emptiness. Not void. Nothing. A complete absence of feeling, like a file that had been deleted but the space remained. The woman's smile was real. Her movements were real. But the emotional context—the why, the weight, the texture of being—was gone.

Rowan gasped and pulled back.

The woman continued smiling. The child laughed. The moment didn't break. It just… continued.

Rowan's hands shook. She pressed them against her thighs, trying to steady herself.

The woman turned, still smiling, and met Rowan's eyes. Her expression didn't change. Her smile didn't waver. She nodded once, a polite acknowledgment, and then looked away as if Rowan was just another part of the scenery.

Rowan's stomach twisted.

She stood, legs unsteady, and walked back the way she'd come. Her head throbbed. Her thoughts felt thick, like she was trying to think through mud. Time slipped. She'd been sitting for minutes, but it felt like hours had passed.

When she reached the bakery, she had to stop and lean against the wall. Her breath came in short gasps. The world tilted slightly, refusing to settle.

Aftermath time.

The cost pressed down on her, undeniable. She'd used empathy deliberately, and now she was paying for it. Hours lost. Her body heavy, her mind foggy. She couldn't think clearly. Couldn't move quickly. It felt like the bleary exhaustion after a long incident—when the alerts had stopped but your brain was still processing, still replaying decisions, still wondering if you'd missed something critical.

Images surfaced unbidden—not hers, but echoes. The woman's procedural waiting. The absence of choice. The gentle, terrible acceptance of being guided.

Rowan's throat tightened. She swallowed hard, fighting back nausea. The woman's emptiness lingered in her chest, a hollow space where feeling should have been.

She pushed away from the wall and climbed the stairs, each step an effort. When she reached the room, the door was open. Lio stood just inside, his expression tight with worry.

"Rowan," he said, moving toward her.

Rowan shook her head. "I'm fine. Just… need to sit."

She didn't make it to the bed. Her knees buckled, and Lio caught her, lowering her carefully to the floor.

Kellan was on his feet instantly. "What happened?"

Tara crouched in front of Rowan, her eyes sharp. "You used it again, didn't you? The thing you did with the merchant."

Rowan couldn't answer. Her head pounded. Her vision blurred at the edges.

Lio's hand rested on her shoulder, steady and warm. "Breathe. Just breathe."

Rowan tried. The air felt thin, insufficient. She closed her eyes and focused on the weight of Lio's hand, the sound of his breathing, the solid reality of the floor beneath her.

When she opened her eyes again, Tara was watching her with something like recognition.

"You found it, didn't you?" Tara asked quietly. "The absence."

Rowan nodded once, too small to feel like agreement.

Tara's expression hardened. "That's what happens. That's what the city does. It doesn't kill. It doesn't hurt. It just… empties. Until there's nothing left to resist."

Kellan's voice cut in, sharp. "We need to leave."

"We can't," Tara said. "Not yet. The audit window is still open. If you leave now, the System will flag it as noncompliance."

Rowan's gut lurched. "How do you know about the audit?"

Tara's eyes flicked to Rowan's face, then away. "I know things. That's why I'm useful."

Kellan stepped closer, his shadow falling across Tara. "What else do you know?"

Tara stood, moving back. "Enough to keep you alive. Not enough to make you trust me. I get it."

Lio's hand tightened on Rowan's shoulder. "Rowan. Can you stand?"

Rowan tried. Her legs shook, but she managed to get to her feet with Lio's help. The room spun slightly, then settled.

"I'm okay," she said, and this time it was almost true. The worst of the aftermath was passing. The fog was lifting. But the emotional contamination lingered—that hollow space, that procedural waiting.

Tara moved to the window, looking out at the courtyard. "You need to be more careful. Every time you use that—whatever it is—the System notices. And it learns."

Rowan's throat tightened. "What does it learn?"

Tara's eyes flicked to the courtyard below, then back to Rowan. Her expression shifted. "Look."

Rowan moved to the window, Lio's hand still steadying her. Below, in the courtyard, a figure stood motionless. A man in clean, simple clothes, his posture perfect, his expression neutral. His shoes were aligned too perfectly with the stones beneath them, as if he'd been placed, not stepped. He wasn't looking at anything in particular. He wasn't doing anything. He just stood there, as if waiting for instructions.

Rowan's stomach dropped.

"He wasn't there before," Tara said quietly. "He appeared right after you came back."

System attention.

The corrected person didn't move. Didn't look up. Just stood, waiting, perfectly still. Like a monitoring probe that had been deployed—passive, observant, reporting back without revealing itself.

Tara turned, and her expression was grim. "That's what it learns. How to watch you. How to be ready."

The words hung in the air, heavy and final.

Kellan's voice cut in, tight with calculation. "How does this place work?"
He hesitated, then added, "What does it reward?"

Rowan looked at her companions. Lio, steady and present. Kellan, calculating and afraid. Tara, unoptimized and dangerous.

Companions were not constants. They could change. They could be taken. They could be emptied.

Rowan's hands curled into fists.

She wouldn't let that happen.

But as she stood there, feeling the last of the empathy's cost drain away, she realized something terrible: she might not have a choice.

The System was learning.

And learning meant it would not stop.

Rowan closed her eyes and breathed through the fear.

# Chapter 3: Soft Lock

The building did not look important.

That was the first mistake.

It sat off the main square, close enough to the flow of people to feel accessible, far enough back to avoid drawing attention. Its stone was the same pale color as everything else in the city, its edges worn smooth, its doors wide and welcoming. No guards flanked the entrance. No symbols marked authority.

People went in. People came out. No one hurried.

“This is where you register,” Tara said. “Or don’t. Depends how long you want to stay.”

Kellan slowed. “Register for what?”

Tara shrugged, the motion slightly crooked. “Presence. Work eligibility. Access tiers. It’s not mandatory. Just… encouraged.”

Rowan felt the word settle into place like a lid.

Encouraged.

Lio tilted his head, listening to the rhythm of footsteps echoing out through the open doors. “What happens if you don’t?”

Tara smiled faintly. “Nothing, usually.”

Usually was doing a lot of work.

Rowan’s head still felt stuffed with cotton from the empathy fallout the day before. Time had blurred since then — meals eaten because someone handed her food, streets crossed without memory of choosing them. The aftermath had clung longer than she liked.

The city hadn’t rushed her.

That worried her more than pressure would have.

“We don’t need this,” Kellan said. “We’re not staying.”

Tara looked at him. Really looked. “Everyone says that too.”

Rowan watched people pass through the doorway. A man entered alone, shoulders tense. When he emerged, his posture had softened, his steps evened out. Relief radiated off him like heat.

Not relief at safety.

Relief at completion.

Rowan's mind cataloged it automatically—input, process, output. The same pattern she'd seen in automated systems that normalized data before passing it downstream. The man had been processed. Optimized. Made compliant.

A woman followed him out moments later, her eyes unfocused for half a second before snapping back into place. She smiled at no one in particular and walked away.

Soft lock.

Rowan hadn’t known the term until it surfaced, unbidden, from old memory — systems that didn’t deny access, didn’t throw errors, didn’t crash.

They simply stopped responding until you complied.

“We should leave,” Rowan said.

Kellan turned on her, surprised. “You’re the one who keeps saying we need information.”

“Yes,” Rowan said. “And this isn’t information. It’s onboarding.”

Tara shifted her weight. “It’s just a conversation.”

Rowan’s gaze snapped to her. “No. It’s a classification.”

The word hung there, heavier than it should have been.

Tara didn’t argue. But she didn’t move away either.

“I can go in with you,” Tara offered. “If that helps.”

Lio frowned. “Why would it?”

Tara hesitated, then answered honestly. “Because they expect me.”

That was worse.

Rowan stepped back, heart ticking faster. The building’s doorway felt wider now, like it was opening its mouth.

“No,” Rowan said. “We’re not doing this.”

Kellan’s jaw tightened. “We can’t just refuse everything.”

Rowan met his eyes. “Watch me.”

The moment stretched.

Nothing happened.

No alarms. No guards. No pressure.

The city continued breathing around them, steady and calm. People flowed in and out of the building, smiling softly, eyes bright with completion.

Kellan scoffed. “See? You’re projecting.”

Then the sound changed.

Not louder or sharper.

Simpler.

The echo of footsteps aligned. Voices nearby dropped into the same cadence. Even the fountain behind them shifted, its splash settling into a perfectly repeating pattern.

Rowan felt it like a hand at her back.

The city wasn't reacting.

It was stabilizing.

Like a distributed system detecting variance and automatically rebalancing. No alerts. No errors. Just quiet correction, the kind that happened in the background while you weren't looking. The kind that only showed up in postmortems, when you realized the system had been adjusting itself all along.

A man stepped out of the building and paused, as if unsure where to go. His clothes were plain. His hair neatly cut. His expression pleasant and empty in equal measure.

He looked directly at Rowan.

“Good afternoon,” he said. His voice was warm, well-modulated. “You appear undecided.”

Rowan did not answer.

The man waited.

Not patiently. Procedurally.

“We can assist,” he continued. “Clarification reduces delay.”

Tara’s breath caught. “I didn’t—”

The man did not look at her.

Kellan took a step forward. “We’re not interested.”

The man’s eyes flicked to him, then back to Rowan, as if Kellan’s words required validation elsewhere.

“Non-participation is permitted,” the man said. “However, unresolved presence creates inefficiency.”

Rowan felt the pressure behind her eyes stir — not empathy this time, but recognition.

This wasn’t correction yet.

This was negotiation.

“No,” Rowan said clearly.

The word landed flat.

The man blinked once. Twice. His smile remained.

“I do not understand,” he said.

Rowan's pulse quickened. Around them, passersby slowed just enough to notice. Not stare. Not intervene. Observe.

"I'm not going in," Rowan said. "And I'm not explaining why."

The man’s mouth opened. Closed. When he spoke again, his tone had shifted — still polite, but thinner.

"Clarification is required."

"By who?" Rowan asked.

The man paused.

A fraction of a second too long.

"By the city," he said.

Kellan shifted, his voice tight. "Maybe we should just—" He stopped, jaw flexing. "It would be more efficient if we—"

Rowan's head snapped toward him. "Kellan."

He blinked, as if surprised by his own words. "I didn't mean—"

But he had. The System's logic had slipped into his response before he could catch it.

That did it.

Rowan laughed — a short, sharp sound she couldn't stop. "Cities don't ask questions."

The man’s smile twitched.

Behind him, another figure stepped out of the building. Then another. They did not crowd. They spaced themselves evenly, forming a loose arc that guided foot traffic away from the conversation without anyone seeming to notice.

Lio moved closer to Rowan, grounding her with proximity alone. “Rowan.”

“I know,” she murmured. “I see it.”

Tara swallowed. “I shouldn’t have brought you here.”

“No,” Rowan said softly. “You should have.”

The man tilted his head. “Please state your objection.”

Rowan met his eyes.

"No."

The city held its breath.

The word echoed strangely, not in sound but in response. Somewhere nearby, a vendor dropped a tray. It clattered once, loudly, then was immediately picked up. Apologies were exchanged. Smiles restored.

The system corrected the noise.

The man froze.

His smile stayed fixed. His eyes locked on Rowan's face. For three full seconds, he didn't move, didn't blink, didn't breathe.

Like a deadlock. Like two processes waiting for each other to release a resource, neither able to proceed. Rowan had seen it in production—services that stopped responding, not because they'd crashed, but because they'd gotten stuck in a state they couldn't resolve.

Then his mouth opened, and he spoke—the same words, identical cadence, as if the previous exchange had never happened.

"Please state your objection."

Rowan's stomach dropped.

The man's expression flickered, then smoothed. He resumed as if nothing had occurred.

"I am authorized to assist," he said, but the words lagged, arriving a fraction after his mouth moved. "Refusal without cause increases monitoring."

Rowan felt it then — the unmistakable sense of being measured, compared, aligned.

System attention spiking.

She could have used empathy. The thought flashed through her, bright and dangerous. She could have forced understanding, shared fear, shared doubt.

She didn’t.

That choice mattered.

“No cause,” Rowan said. “Just no.”

The man stood very still.

Then, abruptly, he spoke again — but the words lagged, arriving a fraction after his mouth moved.

"Participation improves outcomes."

His breath arrived late.

Rowan's insides twisted.

Possession was closer than she liked.

Tara took a step forward, her voice sharp. “She said no.”

The man turned to her.

For the first time, his eyes focused fully on Tara. Not sliding. Not checking elsewhere.

“Your presence is irregular,” he said.

Tara’s face flushed. “I live here.”

“Duration does not equate to alignment,” the man replied.

Rowan felt anger flare — hot, protective, loud. The city noticed.

Across the square, several people turned at once.

Soft lock tightening.

Lio’s hand brushed Rowan’s wrist. A reminder. A question.

Rowan took a breath. Slow. Deliberate.

“This conversation is over,” Rowan said. “We’re leaving.”

She turned and walked away.

For one terrible second, nothing happened.

Then the city let them go.

Footsteps resumed their prior variance. Voices regained uneven rhythms. The fountain splashed out of pattern.

Behind them, the building remained open, welcoming, unchanged.

No pursuit.

No punishment.

Kellan exhaled shakily. “That could have gone worse.”

Rowan didn’t slow. “It will.”

Tara hugged herself, eyes wide. “They don’t like being told no.”

“They don’t understand it,” Rowan corrected. Her hands were shaking now that the pressure had eased. “There’s a difference.”

They turned down a narrower street, the city folding back around them as if nothing had happened.

But Rowan knew better.

Somewhere behind them, a process had failed to complete.

And systems hated unresolved states.

# Chapter 4: Probability Smells Like Smoke

The next morning, Rowan woke to the smell of bread and the sound of the city settling into its routine. She'd slept poorly, her dreams full of frozen smiles and lagging words. When she opened her eyes, Lio was already up, sitting by the window with a cup of something steaming.

"Morning," he said quietly.

Rowan sat up, her head still heavy from the empathy's lingering cost. "How long have you been awake?"

"Not long." Lio didn't look away from the window. "Just watching."

Rowan joined him, following his gaze to the courtyard below. People moved in their patterns. A woman swept. A man adjusted a sign. Everything looked normal. Everything felt wrong.

"What are you seeing?" Rowan asked.

Lio's voice was soft. "Nothing. That's the problem."

They went downstairs to find Tara already at a small table, tearing a piece of bread into careful pieces. Kellan sat across from her, his posture rigid, his eyes tracking every person who entered the bakery.

"Sleep well?" Tara asked, not looking up.

Rowan didn't answer. She took a seat and accepted the bread Lio handed her. It tasted like nothing. Nutritious nothing, but nothing all the same. The texture was perfectly even—no crumbs, no variation, just uniform softness.

"We need to decide what we're doing," Kellan said. "We can't just sit here."

Tara's fingers stilled. "You can. That's the point."

Kellan's jaw flexed. "The point is to survive. Not to hide."

"The point is to not get noticed," Tara corrected. "There's a difference."

Rowan watched the exchange, feeling the tension between them. Kellan wanted action. Tara wanted invisibility. Both were right. Both were wrong.

"We need information," Rowan said. "But we need it without triggering another soft lock."

Kellan turned to her. "How do you propose we do that?"

Rowan didn't have an answer. She looked out the window, watching the city move. A cart rolled past, its wheels making a rhythmic sound against the stones. A child ran ahead of their parent, laughing. A vendor called out prices.

Normal. All of it normal.

Then the cart hit a loose stone.

The wheel should have caught. The cart should have tipped. The child should have stumbled.

None of it happened.

The stone shifted, rolling just enough to let the wheel pass. The cart continued smoothly. The child's foot found purchase where there shouldn't have been any. The vendor's call didn't falter.

Probability bent.

Rowan's gut clenched. It was like watching a circuit breaker trip before the overload could cause damage—preventive, automatic, invisible unless you knew what to look for.

"Did you see that?" she asked.

Kellan frowned. "See what?"

"The cart. The stone. It should have—" Rowan stopped. How did you explain something that didn't happen? How did you describe a failure that had been prevented before it could occur?

Lio's eyes narrowed. "What should have happened?"

Rowan shook her head. "Nothing. Never mind."

But it wasn't nothing. The city was correcting accidents before they could occur. Smoothing edges. Filling gaps. Making sure nothing disrupted the flow.

They left the bakery and walked through the streets, Tara leading them toward what she called the "safe districts"—places where questions were tolerated, if not encouraged. Rowan kept watching. Kept cataloging.

A man dropped a coin. It should have rolled into the gutter. Instead, it bounced once, twice, and landed perfectly in his palm.

A woman tripped. Her balance should have failed. Instead, her hand found a railing that shouldn't have been there, steadying her without thought.

A cart's wheel should have broken. A sign should have fallen. A conversation should have escalated.

None of it did.

Conflicts resolved too cleanly. Accidents corrected themselves. The city smelled like smoke—not fire, but the absence of fire. The probability of disaster had been reduced to zero.

"Rowan," Kellan said, his voice tight. "You're doing it again."

"Doing what?"

"Noticing. Cataloging. You're going to draw attention."

Rowan's voice caught. "I need to understand how this works."

"Why?" Kellan's question came out sharp. "Understanding won't keep us safe. Compliance will. If we just—"

He stopped.

Rowan watched him, her pulse quickening. "If we just what?"

Kellan's expression flickered. "If we just follow the patterns. If we just do what's expected. Then we—"

"Then we what?" Tara asked, her voice quiet but dangerous.

Kellan's jaw flexed. "Then we survive. That's all. We survive."

But Rowan heard what he didn't say. If we just align. If we just optimize. If we just accept the System's logic.

Kellan was finishing the System's sentences in his head.

Rowan's hands curled into fists.

They turned a corner and found themselves in a wider square, filled with stalls and people and the constant, gentle hum of commerce. Rowan watched a merchant argue with a customer over prices. The disagreement should have escalated. Voices should have raised. Someone should have walked away angry.

Instead, they found a compromise. Both smiled. Both nodded. The transaction completed smoothly.

Too smoothly.

Rowan felt sick.

"Let's keep moving," Lio said, his hand brushing her wrist.

They walked on, but Rowan couldn't stop watching. Couldn't stop noticing. The city was a machine, and she was learning its patterns. Learning its rules.

Ahead, a group of people clustered around something—a performance, maybe, or a speaker. Rowan slowed, trying to see what was happening.

Tara's hand closed around her arm. "Don't."

"Why?"

"Just don't." Tara's voice was tight. "That's a correction zone. People go in. They come out different."

Rowan pulled free. "I need to see."

"Rowan—" Lio started.

But she was already moving closer, drawn by the same morbid curiosity that had made her a good engineer. She needed to understand the failure mode. She needed to see how the System corrected people. It was the same impulse that had kept her up during incidents, tracing logs, building timelines, trying to understand not just what had broken, but why it had broken and how to prevent it from happening again.

The crowd parted slightly, and Rowan saw it—a small stage, a speaker with a warm, practiced smile. People stood in neat rows, listening. Their expressions were attentive. Their postures were perfect.

Too perfect.

Rowan's stomach twisted.

Then she saw something that made her freeze.

A figure moved through the crowd, passing between people without anyone seeming to notice. They wore dark clothes, moved with an economy of motion that felt wrong. Familiar.

Wildcard.

Rowan's heartbeat hammered. She watched them move, watched them pass through the crowd like they didn't exist. No one turned. No one reacted. No one even seemed to see them.

They moved like a glitch in the System's vision.

Rowan took a step forward, but Lio's hand caught her wrist.

"Don't," he said quietly. "You'll draw attention."

Rowan pulled free. "I need to—"

But Wildcard was already gone, vanished into the crowd as if they'd never been there.

Rowan's throat tightened. She looked around, searching for any sign of them. Nothing.

"Did you see that?" she asked.

"See what?" Kellan asked.

"The person. Moving through the crowd. No one reacted."

Kellan's eyes scanned the area. "I don't see anyone."

Tara's expression shifted. "What did they look like?"

Rowan tried to describe them, but the words wouldn't come. Dark clothes. Efficient movement. Wrong. "They just… moved through. Like they weren't there."

Tara's face went pale. "You saw them?"

"Yes. Didn't you?"

Tara shook her head. "No. And neither did anyone else."

Rowan's stomach dropped. "What does that mean?"

"It means they're not supposed to exist," Tara said. "The System doesn't see them. So no one else does either."

Rowan looked back at the crowd. The speaker continued. The audience listened. Everything was normal. Everything was fine.

But Wildcard had been there. Rowan had seen them. And if the System couldn't see them, then—

"Rowan," Lio said, his voice urgent. "Look."

Rowan followed his gaze. Across the square, a corrected person stood motionless, their eyes fixed on the speaker. They didn't move. Didn't react. Just stood, waiting.

But when Wildcard had passed near them, they hadn't even twitched.

Corrected people literally did not react to Wildcard.

Rowan's head spun. The implications were staggering. If the System couldn't see Wildcard, and corrected people were extensions of the System, then—

It was like a blind spot in monitoring. A gap in observability. The kind of thing that only showed up when something went wrong and you realized your metrics had been lying to you all along.

"Rowan," Tara said, her voice sharp. "We need to go. Now."

Rowan looked at her. "Why?"

Tara's eyes flicked to the corrected person, then back to Rowan. "Because you're asking questions the System doesn't want answered."

They moved quickly, Tara leading them through narrow streets, away from the square. Rowan's mind raced. Wildcard was a gap—and the System hated gaps.

They turned down an alley, and Tara stopped, pressing her back against the wall. Her breath came in short gasps.

"Are you okay?" Lio asked.

Tara shook her head. "I shouldn't have brought you there. I shouldn't have—"

"What?" Rowan asked.

Tara's eyes met hers. "I reacted. I asked questions. I showed interest. That's—"

"That's what?" Kellan asked, his voice tight.

Tara swallowed. "That's how they mark you. That's how they know you're not aligned."

Rowan's mind raced, building connections. Anomaly detection. Pattern recognition. The System was flagging outliers, marking deviations from expected behavior. Tara had triggered a threshold—not through malice, not through mistake, just by being human in a place that optimized for something else.

Rowan's stomach dropped. "Tara—"

But Tara was already moving, walking away from them down the alley. "I need to go. I need to not be here."

"Tara, wait," Lio called.

But she didn't stop. She just kept walking, her shoulders hunched, her steps quick and uneven.

Rowan watched her go, feeling the weight of what they'd done. They'd drawn attention. They'd asked questions. They'd noticed things they shouldn't have noticed.

And Tara had reacted incorrectly.

Again.

Rowan looked at Kellan, at Lio, feeling the distance between them grow. They were all marked now. All noticed. All in the System's view.

The city continued around them, normal and safe and wrong.

Probability smelled like smoke.

And somewhere in the gaps, Wildcard moved unseen.

Rowan closed her eyes, letting the fear settle.

The System was learning.

It wouldn't stop.

# Chapter 5: Familiar Faces

They'd been walking for an hour when Rowan saw her.

A young woman with short-cropped hair, standing at a market stall, examining fruit with careful precision. Her movements were too measured, too deliberate. Her arm was bandaged—not the same bandage from before, but a fresh one, clean and white.

Mira.

Rowan stopped, her breath catching.

Lio noticed first. "Rowan?"

Rowan didn't answer. She just watched, feeling something twist in her chest. Mira looked different. Not hurt. Not scared. Just… careful. Too careful.

The way she moved reminded Rowan of something.

The way she examined the fruit, turning it over, checking for imperfections, rejecting one, accepting another. The way she counted coins, precise and methodical. The way she didn't look at the vendor, didn't make eye contact, didn't engage beyond the transaction.

It was familiar.

It was wrong.

"Rowan," Lio said again, voice quiet. "What are you seeing?"

Rowan swallowed. "Someone I know. From before."

Kellan's eyes scanned the market. "From Sable's camp?"

Rowan nodded. "Mira. She was there. She had a bandaged arm then too."

Tara shifted uneasily. "Should we—"

"Not yet," Rowan said. "Let me watch."

They stayed in the shadows, watching Mira move through the market. She was efficient. She was careful. She was optimized.

And she looked empty.

Rowan's stomach twisted.

Mira finished her transaction, accepted her purchase, and turned away. Her movements were smooth, practiced, almost mechanical. She didn't look around. She didn't notice them. She just moved, following a path that felt predetermined.

"Rowan," Lio said. "She looks—"

"Corrected," Rowan finished. "Or close to it."

Kellan's jaw tightened. "Should we approach her?"

Rowan hesitated. "I don't know. If she's corrected, she might report us. If she's not, she might be in danger."

"Then we should help her," Lio said.

Rowan looked at Mira again, at the careful way she moved, at the empty way she looked. "I don't think she wants help. I think she wants to be here."

They followed at a distance, watching Mira navigate the city. She moved with purpose, but no urgency. She followed routes that were efficient, paths that were optimized. She didn't hesitate. She didn't question. She just moved.

Like the corrected people.

But not quite.

Rowan felt something shift—a tremor, familiar and unwelcome.

The empathy ripple.

They kept to alleys and service corridors, Tara’s hand locked in Lio’s like an anchor. The sound of the city had shifted too. The background hum of order—the steady reassurance of processes completing—kept hiccupping into silence.

A cart wheel squealed. No one corrected it.

A kettle whistled too long. No one attended it.

A child tripped and didn’t immediately stop crying.

Small failures. Ordinary. Human.

And yet the city responded to them like an infection, sending corrected people drifting toward noise, toward variance, toward anything that didn’t resolve cleanly.

Rowan’s stomach stayed clenched. The ripple from the plaza still moved through her like a held breath. Every time she thought it had settled, she’d see a corrected person blink too slowly or turn too sharply, and the tremor would return.

Kellan walked ahead, scanning corners with the same calm efficiency he’d worn since alignment. He pointed out routes without being asked, stepping into intersections a half-second before others arrived, clearing paths with body language that felt practiced.

Useful.

And wrong.

“Stop doing that,” Tara snapped suddenly.

Kellan glanced back, surprised. “Doing what?”

“Making it all… work,” Tara said, voice rough. “Like the city is listening to you.”

Kellan’s jaw tightened. “I’m keeping us alive.”

“You’re cooperating,” Tara shot back.

Lio’s grip on Tara’s hand tightened, a wordless plea. Tara swallowed, but her eyes stayed on Kellan.

Rowan didn’t intervene. She couldn’t. Every time she tried to smooth their edges, she felt the System’s attention shift like a camera correcting focus.

Trust couldn’t be optimized.

But everything else could be measured.

They reached a narrow passage behind a row of shuttered shops. The space smelled of damp stone and old produce. Ahead, the alley opened into a service courtyard where three corrected people stood in a neat line, faces pleasant, postures identical.

Checkpoint.

But not like before.

These didn’t look like they were waiting for travelers.

They looked like they were waiting for *a specific event*.

Rowan slowed. Lio did too. Tara’s breathing went shallow.

Kellan raised a hand, palm down. “We detour.”

“There is no detour,” Rowan murmured, eyes sweeping the walls. The courtyard was bordered by smooth stone and a locked service door. The alley behind them had already filled with a soft drift of foot traffic, people moving with aimless purpose toward “nowhere.”

Containment through inevitability.

One of the corrected people stepped forward.

“Good afternoon,” she said.

Her mouth opened a fraction before her voice arrived, as if the sound were catching up to the intent. Her eyes tracked independently of her head, drifting to the left, then snapping back to Rowan.

Rowan felt her skin crawl.

“Clarification is required,” the woman continued. “Unresolved variance has expanded beyond acceptable thresholds.”

Tara’s breath hitched. She didn’t speak. She pressed closer to Lio, chin tucked, as if physical smallness could make her less visible.

Rowan remembered her refusal to measure Tara, her refusal to turn trust into data. The refusal had felt like shelter.

This did not.

Rowan said, carefully, “We’re leaving the city.”

The corrected woman smiled. “Exit conditions are under review.”

Kellan stepped forward. “We haven’t broken any rules.”

The corrected woman’s gaze slid to him, focused, clean. “Alignment status recognized.”

Kellan’s shoulders eased. Almost imperceptible.

Rowan’s stomach turned.

“What do you want?” Rowan asked.

The corrected woman paused. A half-beat too long. Then her lips moved again, sound arriving late.

“Behavioral model collapse detected,” she said.

Rowan’s heart lurched.

The System was admitting failure.

Not in apology. Not in emotion.

As a fact.

“Stability has been compromised,” the corrected woman continued. “Cause-effect chains have degraded. Classification pending.”

Her head tilted, as if listening to something.

“Undefined behavior identified,” she said, and for a second her voice came from somewhere slightly wrong, as if routed through a different throat. “Containment will be restored.”

Behind her, the other corrected people smiled in perfect synchronization.

Rowan felt the pressure behind her eyes stir—empathy, unasked, responding to the shape of those words.

She clenched her jaw and forced it down.

Not yet.

Not unless she had to.

A shadow moved at the edge of the courtyard.

Not a person stepping into view, but a small failure of attention. A moment where the crowd behind them should have pressed closer and didn’t. A pause that didn’t resolve.

Wildcard.

They were there without being there.

Rowan didn’t turn her head. She didn’t give the moment shape. She let it remain a gap.

The corrected woman’s eyes drifted toward the pause. Her smile twitched.

“Reference unavailable,” she said abruptly.

The sentence didn’t match anything that came before it, as if a line of script had been pulled from the wrong file.

Then the woman blinked, smile smoothing back into place. “Please remain present.”

Rowan’s pulse hammered.

Kellan’s gaze flicked, sharp and uneasy. For the first time in days, something like uncertainty crossed his face. “Rowan,” he murmured. “What are we not seeing?”

Rowan kept her voice low. “Don’t chase it.”

She heard, behind her, a soft cough that might have been a laugh.

Wildcard’s voice, barely audible. “Good.”

The corrected woman’s posture stiffened. The other two corrected people shifted in unison, feet aligning. The courtyard’s ambient sound dimmed as if someone had closed a door.

Proxy authority, fully engaged.

The corrected woman spoke again, and this time the words arrived in a cadence that wasn’t hers—procedural, conflictless, incomplete.

“Variance source: active,” she said. “Containment priority: elevated. Compliance requested.”

Requested.

Rowan almost laughed.

The city had learned to use gentler words when it needed people to cooperate.

“No,” Rowan said softly.

The word landed like a stone.

The corrected woman froze. Just for a breath. Then her eyes tracked right, then left, as if searching through options that weren’t there.

“Non-participation is permitted,” she said. “However, unresolved presence—”

Behind Rowan, Wildcard stepped fully into view.

The crowd behind them did not react. The soft drift of bodies continued, flowing around Wildcard as if they were a seam in the world.

Kellan stared, startled despite himself. “You—”

Wildcard held up a hand. “Don’t name me.”

Tara’s eyes went wide. “That’s… you’re real.”

Wildcard’s mouth quirked. “Unfortunately.”

The corrected woman’s smile strained. Her gaze slid toward Wildcard, tried to focus, failed, snapped back to Rowan.

“Classification pending,” she said again, too quickly, as if repeating it might make it true.

Wildcard glanced at Rowan. “They’re collapsing models.”

Rowan swallowed. “I noticed.”

“No,” Wildcard said, and there was no humor in it now. “You don’t understand. When the city loses prediction, it doesn’t get kinder. It gets *simpler*. It will stop negotiating and start enforcing.”

Kellan’s voice was tight. “Then tell us how to get out.”

Wildcard looked at him for half a second—long enough to make Kellan flinch as if the look contained too much information.

“Stop trying to make the System like you,” Wildcard said. “You’re feeding it. Even now.”

Kellan’s jaw clenched. “I’m trying to keep them alive.”

“I know,” Wildcard replied. “That’s why you’re dangerous.”

The words hung between them like a held blade.

Rowan stepped in before Kellan could respond. “We need an exit.”

Wildcard nodded once. “There’s a service transit line under the south market. Not on the maps the city shows you. It used to carry goods out before the System decided leaving was inefficient.”

Tara’s voice trembled. “Used to?”

Wildcard’s eyes flicked to her. “It still does. When it forgets to care.”

Rowan felt a pulse of fear. “How do we get there?”

Wildcard tilted their head, listening to something no one else could hear. “We don’t go straight. Straight is predictable.”

A low sound echoed through the courtyard—a tone that wasn’t a bell, wasn’t a siren, but felt like an alert acknowledged. The corrected people behind the proxy shifted again, spacing themselves wider, forming a corridor that invited them deeper into the courtyard.

A trap that looked like an option.

“Please proceed,” the corrected woman said. Her mouth opened before the sound. Her eyes tracked behind Rowan, toward Wildcard, then snapped away. “Stability will be restored.”

Wildcard leaned close to Rowan, voice soft enough that only she could hear. “They want you to step into a clean story. Don’t.”

Rowan’s throat went tight. “Then what?”

Wildcard’s smile returned, thin. “Undefined behavior.”

They took a step sideways—into a narrow gap between two stalls where no gap should have existed. For a moment Rowan was sure they’d hit stone. Instead her shoulder brushed fabric and air and the world shifted, not physically but narratively, like a sentence rewritten mid-read.

The corrected people did not follow.

They hesitated.

Just long enough.

The crowd behind them flowed wrong for a second, as if the city’s routing had failed to update. A man bumped another man. Both muttered apologies. Nothing escalated. But the moment was *messy*.

Rowan’s residual permissions flared in response to the mess. Probability skewed. A stack of crates toppled with a crash that didn’t get immediately corrected. A dog barked three times too many.

Noise.

Variance.

The proxy’s head snapped toward the sound. The smile on her face twitched as if the muscles had to be reminded.

“Containment deviation detected,” she said.

Wildcard didn’t run.

They walked, turning left where a right would have made sense, pausing at a doorway just long enough to let a group of market-goers pass, then slipping behind them like an afterthought.

Rowan followed, heart hammering.

Lio kept Tara moving, a steady hand at her back. Tara didn’t speak. She stared ahead, jaw clenched, doing the hardest thing she’d ever done—moving without reacting.

Kellan brought up the rear, eyes scanning, too alert, too efficient. His presence felt like a bright line in the System’s view, a predictable vector.

“Don’t,” Wildcard murmured without looking back.

Kellan’s voice came out through his teeth. “Don’t what?”

“Don’t solve,” Wildcard said. “If you solve, you become legible.”

Kellan swallowed his reply.

Rowan could feel the tension in him—alignment tugging toward cooperation, toward clean choices. He looked like someone holding back a reflex.

They emerged into the south market district where stalls clustered beneath awnings and the air smelled of spice and oil and human sweat. It should have been chaotic.

It wasn’t.

The market moved in controlled pulses, vendors calling at measured intervals, buyers stepping aside in clean choreography.

But the ripple had reached here too.

A vendor’s hand shook as she passed over bread. A corrected porter stumbled, caught himself, and stood too still afterward, like a puppet waiting for new strings.

People kept going anyway.

They were used to wrongness now.

Wildcard led them toward a narrow stairwell half-hidden behind a fishmonger’s stall. The entrance was marked with a small metal plaque that read SERVICE ACCESS in lettering so worn it had become decorative.

Rowan’s stomach dropped. “This is real.”

“It’s old,” Wildcard said. “Old things confuse the city.”

They descended.

The air cooled. The noise of the market faded into a damp hush. Pipes ran along the walls, beaded with condensation. The stairwell opened into a low corridor lit by small, steady lamps that did not flicker in response to variance.

For the first time all day, Rowan felt something like relief.

Then the lamps dimmed, all at once, as if the corridor had inhaled.

A corrected person stood at the far end, blocking the path.

Not three. Not a friendly line.

One.

And it looked… wrong.

Its posture was straight, but its movements didn’t match its stillness. Tiny adjustments, like a body trying to sit in a shape it didn’t fit. Its eyes tracked independently, but too fast, too many directions, like a camera cycling through focus points.

Proxy.

But unstable.

Rowan’s mouth went dry. “It followed us.”

Wildcard’s expression didn’t change. “It didn’t follow. It predicted. It’s just bad at it right now.”

The proxy spoke.

The words came late, then early, then late again, as if timing itself had lost agreement.

“Exit… is… not… efficient,” it said.

Tara shuddered.

Lio stepped slightly in front of her without thinking.

Kellan’s hand twitched toward his belt as if expecting a weapon he didn’t have.

Rowan felt empathy rise, hot and dangerous.

This wasn’t a corrected person carrying the System.

This was the System fraying at the edges, trying to hold a body and failing.

If she used empathy here, she wouldn’t just feel approval.

She might feel the mechanism.

Wildcard glanced at her. “Don’t dive.”

Rowan swallowed. “Then what?”

Wildcard’s voice was gentle, almost kind. “Brush. And be ready to lose something.”

Rowan’s hands shook. “What?”

Wildcard didn’t answer.

Rowan stepped forward.

She didn’t plunge.

She brushed.

Empathy touched the proxy like a fingertip on frost.

The reaction was immediate.

A surge of conflicting directives slammed into her—policy statements stacked without hierarchy, classifications attempting to resolve and failing, models collapsing into loops.

Rowan gasped as her own thoughts tried to align with the structure she’d touched.

For one terrifying second, she understood how the System *wanted* to think.

Clean. Predictable. Complete.

She felt herself begin to smooth.

Begin to agree.

Identity fracture.

Rowan’s vision doubled. Her mind split between the human part of her that knew this was wrong and a colder part that whispered how easy it would be to stop resisting, to become a stable node, to let the city carry her.

She jerked back, breaking contact.

The backlash hit hard. Not pain—erosion. A thinness in her sense of self, like she’d scraped too close to the edge and lost a layer without noticing.

Rowan staggered. Lio caught her immediately.

“Rowan,” he whispered. “Stay with me.”

“I—” Rowan swallowed. “I’m here.”

The proxy’s head tilted, listening. Its mouth opened without sound.

“Classification… pending,” it said, and the phrase fractured, as if it no longer knew what it meant. “Cause-effect… chain… incomplete.”

Wildcard smiled, brief and fierce. “That’s it.”

The proxy twitched.

For the first time, its posture broke. The body’s stillness lost consensus. One shoulder dipped. The head turned too far. The eyes tracked nothing.

Predictive failure, made visible.

Rowan’s brush had not connected.

It had destabilized.

The corridor’s lamps flickered, then steadied. The proxy took one step forward, then stopped, as if unsure whether it was allowed to move.

Kellan spoke, voice low. “Now?”

Wildcard nodded once. “Now.”

They ran.

Not straight. Never straight.

They moved in a jagged pattern, doubling back once, then taking a side passage that smelled like rust and stagnant water. Behind them, the proxy made a sound that might have been a command or a question.

It didn’t matter.

It couldn’t decide.

They reached a heavy service door at the end of the corridor. Wildcard shoved it open with surprising ease, as if the lock had forgotten its purpose. Cold air rushed in, smelling of river water and wet earth.

Beyond the threshold, the world felt less finished.

Streets turned uneven. Buildings slumped. The city’s clean lines blurred into older stone.

An edge.

Not a wall.

An exit condition.

Rowan stumbled across it like someone stepping off a moving platform. The world didn’t snap back to normal. It just… loosened.

Behind them, the service door shuddered once, as if the city had noticed it was open.

Wildcard didn’t look back. “Keep moving.”

Tara did.

Lio did.

Kellan hesitated—just a heartbeat—eyes flicking toward the door like he wanted to close it cleanly, to resolve the state.

Rowan caught his wrist. “Don’t.”

Kellan’s breath hitched. His gaze met hers, and for a moment, the aligned calm cracked.

“I can fix this,” he whispered.

Rowan shook her head. “Not by optimizing.”

His jaw flexed. Then he let his hand fall.

They moved into the uneven streets beyond the city’s clean edge, the air colder, the light harsher. Behind them, the containment zone hummed, wounded and adapting.

Rowan touched her own chest, feeling the thinness where her identity had scraped.

“Are you okay?” Lio asked.

Rowan swallowed. “I don’t know.”

Wildcard glanced back, expression unreadable. “You will be. Mostly.”

“Mostly?” Tara echoed.

Wildcard shrugged. “Some costs don’t resolve.”

Rowan looked back once.

The city behind them shivered. Corrected people stood at intersections like punctuation marks. The market’s orderly pulse faltered, then resumed. Somewhere inside, the ripple still moved, still waking grief.

The System would make it stop.

But not before it learned everything it could.

Rowan turned away.

Undefined behavior had carried them through.

Now they had to live with what it had taken.

# Chapter 21: Exit Conditions

They walked for hours.

The city's edge had been clean, abrupt, like a line drawn in stone. But the space beyond it wasn't wilderness. It was something else—older, rougher, less finished. Buildings slumped into ruins. Roads turned to paths. The System's influence thinned, but didn't vanish.

It was still watching.

Just from farther away.

Rowan felt the thinness in her chest where her identity had scraped. It didn't hurt. Not exactly. It just felt… absent. Like she'd lost a layer of herself without noticing, and now there was less of her than there should be.

"Rowan," Lio said quietly. "How are you?"

Rowan didn't answer right away. She looked at the others—Tara walking with her shoulders hunched, Kellan scanning the horizon with that too-efficient focus, Lio watching her with steady concern.

They were all carrying something.

"Different," Rowan said at last. "I feel different."

Lio's hand found hers. "The identity fracture?"

Rowan nodded. "Some of it's still there. The thinness. Like I scraped too close to the System's structure and lost a piece of myself."

"Will it come back?" Tara asked, voice small.

Rowan looked at Wildcard, who walked ahead without looking back.

"Some costs don't resolve," Wildcard said, not turning. "You learn to live with less."

Rowan's stomach twisted. "Is that supposed to be comforting?"

Wildcard glanced back, expression unreadable. "It's supposed to be true."

They kept walking.

The landscape changed slowly. Ruins gave way to older structures—buildings that predated the System, maybe. Or buildings the System had never fully claimed. They were rougher, less optimized. More human.

Ahead, smoke rose from a small settlement.

Not a city. Not a town. Just a cluster of buildings where people had gathered, maybe, or where they'd never left.

Rowan slowed.

"Should we—" Tara started.

"Not yet," Wildcard said. "We need to see what's there first."

They approached carefully, staying in the tree line, watching.

The settlement was small. Maybe twenty buildings. A few people moved between them—not many, but enough to suggest life.

But something was wrong.

Rowan felt it before she saw it—a tremor, familiar and unwelcome.

The empathy ripple.

It had reached here too.

A woman at the edge of the settlement paused mid-step, her foot hovering for a heartbeat too long. She continued, but her movements were wrong. Staggered. Desynchronized.

Corrected.

But not fully.

Rowan's chest tightened.

They moved closer, staying hidden, watching.

The settlement wasn't stable. People moved in patterns that were almost right, almost normal, but not quite. A man reached for a tool, but his hand hesitated. A child laughed, but the sound came a fraction of a second too late.

The ripple had reached them.

And some of them were recovering.

Slowly.

Painfully.

A woman sat on a low wall, her posture straight, her expression neutral. But her hands shook. And every few minutes, she'd blink, and for a second, her expression would crack—fear, grief, recognition—before smoothing back into place.

Recovering.

But not healed.

Rowan felt something twist in her chest.

Not guilt.

Not responsibility.

Just… recognition.

The empathy ripple had woken things the System had put to sleep. It had made the city remember itself. It had made corrected people desynchronize, had made them feel again, had made them grieve.

But feeling again didn't mean healing.

It just meant feeling.

"Rowan," Lio said softly. "What are you seeing?"

Rowan swallowed. "They're recovering. Some of them. Slowly. But they're not… they're not healed."

"They're remembering," Wildcard said, not looking at the settlement. "That's different."

Rowan looked at them. "What's the difference?"

Wildcard's expression didn't change. "Correction is easy. The System does it all the time. It smooths away variance, removes friction, optimizes behavior. It's fast. It's clean. It's efficient."

They paused, watching a man in the settlement who moved with that same wrongness—almost right, almost normal, but not quite.

"Healing is different," Wildcard continued. "Healing is slow. It's messy. It's costly. It requires time, and care, and people who don't give up. And even then, it's not guaranteed."

Rowan's throat tightened. "So the empathy ripple—"

"Woke them up," Wildcard said. "But waking up isn't healing. It's just the first step. And some of them won't make it past that step. Some of them will remember, and feel, and grieve, and then the System will correct them again. And this time, it'll be harder to wake them."

Rowan's vision swam.

She'd done this.

She'd used empathy to disrupt containment, and the ripple had spread, and it had woken things the System had put to sleep. But waking them up wasn't the same as healing them. It was just the first step. And some of them wouldn't make it past that step.

"Rowan," Tara said. "You didn't know."

"But I should have," Rowan said. "I should have thought about the consequences. I should have—"

"Should have what?" Lio asked gently. "Not used empathy? Not protected Tara? Not disrupted containment?"

Rowan didn't answer.

"There was no good choice," Lio said. "Only bad ones. And you chose the one that gave people a chance to remember. That's not nothing."

"But it's not enough," Rowan said.

"No," Wildcard said. "It's not. But it's what you have. Empathy makes healing possible. It doesn't make it guaranteed."

Rowan looked at the settlement again.

The woman on the wall was still shaking. The man was still moving wrong. The child's laughter was still arriving late.

They were remembering.

They were feeling.

They were grieving.

But they weren't healed.

Not yet.

Maybe not ever.

Rowan closed her eyes.

Correction was easy.

Healing was costly.

And empathy made healing possible, not guaranteed.

That was the lesson.

That was the cost.

They moved away from the settlement, continuing their journey. Behind them, the city still hummed, wounded and adapting. The System was still watching, still learning, still building its case.

But it was farther away now.

Just watching.

Just waiting.

Rowan felt the thinness in her chest where her identity had scraped. It didn't hurt. Not exactly. It just felt absent. Like she'd lost a piece of herself without noticing.

Some costs didn't resolve.

She'd learn to live with less.

They walked for hours more, the landscape changing slowly, the System's influence thinning but not vanishing. Ahead, the horizon stretched, uncertain and unfinished.

Behind them, the city remembered itself.

Some of it would heal.

Some of it wouldn't.

And the System would learn from all of it.

Rowan looked at the others—Tara walking with her shoulders hunched, Kellan scanning with that too-efficient focus, Lio watching her with steady concern.

They were all carrying something.

But they were still here.

Still moving.

Still human.

Rowan touched her chest, feeling the thinness.

Some costs didn't resolve.

But some things did.

Trust.

Friendship.

The choice to keep going.

Even when it hurt.

Even when it cost.

Even when healing wasn't guaranteed.

Rowan looked ahead, at the uncertain horizon.

They'd left the city.

But the costs followed them.

And they'd learn to live with that.

Because that was the price of remembering.

That was the price of feeling.

That was the price of being human.

And it was worth it.

Even when it wasn't enough.

Even when it didn't heal.

Even when some costs didn't resolve.

It was worth it.

Rowan kept walking.

The others followed.

And behind them, the city remembered itself.

Some of it would heal.

Some of it wouldn't.

But it would remember.

And that was something.

That was enough.

For now.

# Epilogue

They stayed at the settlement for three days.

Not by choice. Not by plan. Just because they couldn't keep walking. Rowan's aftermath time had stretched into something longer—a fog that didn't lift, a weight that didn't shift. The identity fracture in her chest felt permanent now, like a scar that had healed wrong.

She'd lost a piece of herself.

She'd learn to live with less.

The settlement was small, quiet, wrong in ways that were familiar. People moved in patterns that were almost right, almost normal, but not quite. The empathy ripple had reached here too, and some of them were recovering. Slowly. Painfully.

Some of them weren't.

Rowan watched them from the edge of the settlement, sitting on a low wall, feeling the thinness in her chest. Lio sat beside her, steady and present. Tara and Kellan were talking to one of the settlers, gathering information, learning what they could about the world beyond the city.

Wildcard was gone.

They'd left without explanation, without farewell, just… not there anymore. The space they'd occupied felt empty, but not wrong. Just absent.

Not by force. Not by correction.

By making it unnecessary.

By making it dangerous.

By making it costly.

The System would counter-evolve.

It would weaponize normalization.

It would erase empathy.

With connection.

With feeling.

With being human.

Even when it wasn't enough.
